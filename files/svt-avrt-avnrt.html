Supraventricular tachycardia: AVNRT, AVRT & Verapamil

Clinical overview with fact-check of conversation content

SVT classification snapshot

Most common SVT
AVNRT
~60% of all SVT. Circuit entirely within or around the AV node using slow & fast pathways.
Second most common
AVRT
~30% of SVT. Requires AV node + accessory pathway. Associated with WPW when pathway conducts anterogradely.
Acute termination
Adenosine first
Half-life <30 s. Verapamil second-line. Both block AV node — contraindicated in pre-excited AF.

Circuit anatomy

Critical rule: Never give verapamil, diltiazem, digoxin, or adenosine in pre-excited AF (WPW + AF) — removing AV nodal brake accelerates conduction down the accessory pathway → risk of VF.

AVRT subtypes

More common (~95%)
Orthodromic AVRT
Antegrade via AV node → retrograde via accessory pathway. Narrow complex. Retrograde P after QRS.
Rare (~5%)
Antidromic AVRT
Antegrade via accessory pathway → retrograde via AV node. Wide complex — can mimic VT. Delta wave morphology throughout.

Mechanism & use

Non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker
Blocks L-type Ca²⁺ channels in AV nodal cells → slows conduction velocity + increases refractory period → breaks reentrant circuit in AVNRT and orthodromic AVRT. Does NOT affect accessory pathway conduction.

Acute IV dosing

Initial dose 2.5–5 mg IV slow push over 2 min
Repeat dose 5–10 mg IV every 15–30 min if needed
Maximum ~20 mg total

Contraindications

WPW + AF / pre-excited AF: Blocks AV node but not AP → all impulses funnel through AP → rapid ventricular response → VF risk.

Statements checked against ESC / ACC guidelines and standard references

AVNRT is the most common SVT (~60%), AVRT second (~30%) — accurate. ESC 2019 SVT guidelines confirm AVNRT as most frequent, AVRT second, with AVRT strongly associated with accessory pathways.
ESC 2019 Guidelines for SVT Management; Brugada et al.
In typical AVNRT, the P wave is buried in or at the very end of the QRS (RP <70 ms) — confirmed. Retrograde atrial activation is near-simultaneous with ventricular activation in slow-fast AVNRT.
Katritsis & Josephson, JACC 2016; standard EP textbooks
Orthodromic AVRT RP interval 70–110 ms — correct. Retrograde conduction via AP takes time, so P wave appears distinctly after QRS, longer RP than AVNRT.
Wellens & Conover; ESC SVT guideline
Verapamil IV: 2.5–5 mg initial, repeat 5–10 mg, max ~20 mg — dosing is accurate per standard references and ESC/AHA guidelines.
ESC SVT 2019; BNF; ACC/AHA SVT guideline 2015
Verapamil in WPW + AF → VF risk — critically correct. Blocking the AV node increases conduction preferentially via the accessory pathway, enabling very rapid ventricular response (RR interval ≤250 ms) → degeneration into VF.
ACC/AHA 2015; Akhtar et al.; Wellens 1987 landmark paper
Verapamil in VT causes cardiovascular collapse — accurate. Verapamil's negative inotropy + vasodilation is extremely dangerous in haemodynamically significant VT. "Never give verapamil to wide complex tachycardia of unknown origin" is a guideline-level teaching point.
Stewart et al., Ann Intern Med 1986; ACC/AHA 2015
Adenosine half-life <30 seconds — accurate. Adenosine's plasma half-life is approximately 10 seconds, with effects lasting under 30 seconds. This is what makes it both diagnostic and therapeutically safe for most SVTs.
DiMarco et al.; product label; standard pharmacology
Adenosine described as "relatively safer" in wide complex tachycardia of unknown origin — nuanced. This reflects common clinical teaching but is not universally endorsed. The ACC/AHA guideline notes adenosine can be used diagnostically in stable wide complex tachycardia if SVT-with-aberrancy is suspected, but VT is not a primary indication. Use with defibrillator available.
ACC/AHA 2015 SVT guideline, section 9.3
Antidromic AVRT is rare (~5% of AVRT) — correct. Antidromic tachycardia, where conduction travels antegrade down the accessory pathway, accounts for a small minority of AVRT cases.
Josephson, Clinical Cardiac Electrophysiology, 5th ed.
